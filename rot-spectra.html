Rotational Energy Levels

For a rigid rotor diatomic molecule:

$$E_J = hcBJ(J+1), \quad J = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

The rotational constant $B$ is related to the moment of inertia:

$$B = \frac{h}{8\pi^2 c I}, \quad I = \mu r_e^2$$

Selection Rule & Line Spacing

Rotational transitions obey $\Delta J = \pm 1$. Each absorption line corresponds to $J \to J+1$:

$$\tilde{\nu}_{J\to J+1} = 2B(J+1)$$

Lines are equally spaced by $2B$, so measuring the spacing directly gives $B$ and thus the bond length $r_e$.

Boltzmann Populations

The population of level $J$ at temperature $T$ is:

$$N_J \propto (2J+1)\, e^{-hcBJ(J+1)/k_BT}$$

The most populated level is at $J_{\max} \approx \sqrt{k_BT / 2hcB} - \tfrac{1}{2}$. Higher temperature shifts the population maximum to higher $J$.

Determining Bond Lengths

From a measured $B$ (in cm⁻¹), the bond length is found via:

$$r_e = \sqrt{\frac{h}{8\pi^2 c \mu B}}$$

This is one of the most precise methods for measuring internuclear distances. Try observing how heavy molecules (CO, N₂) have small $B$ and dense line patterns, while light hydrides (HF, HCl) have large $B$ and widely spaced lines.
