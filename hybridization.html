Mathematical Foundation

Hybrid orbitals arise from linear combinations of atomic orbitals (LCAO). Each hybrid $|\psi_h\rangle$ is a normalised superposition of the central-atom $|s\rangle$, $|p_x\rangle$, $|p_y\rangle$, and $|p_z\rangle$ orbitals:

$$|\psi_h\rangle = c_s|s\rangle + c_{p_x}|p_x\rangle + c_{p_y}|p_y\rangle + c_{p_z}|p_z\rangle$$

Normalisation requires $c_s^2+c_{p_x}^2+c_{p_y}^2+c_{p_z}^2=1$. The squared coefficient $c_s^2$ gives the s character of the hybrid, which controls electronegativity, bond angles, and acidity.

sp

Linear · 180° · 50% s
$$|\psi_1\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|s\rangle+\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|p_z\rangle$$ $$|\psi_2\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|s\rangle-\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|p_z\rangle$$ Examples: C≡C, CO2, HCN

sp²

Trigonal planar · 120° · 33% s
$$|\psi_1\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}|s\rangle+\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{3}}|p_z\rangle$$ Examples: C=C, BF3, NO3−

sp³

Tetrahedral · 109.47° · 25% s
$$|\psi_1\rangle=\tfrac{1}{2}(|s\rangle+|p_x\rangle+|p_y\rangle+|p_z\rangle)$$ Examples: CH4, NH3, H2O
