IR Activity Selection Rule

A vibrational mode is IR active only if it produces a change in the molecular dipole moment:

$$\left(\frac{\partial \mu}{\partial Q}\right)_0 \neq 0$$

where $Q$ is the normal coordinate and $\mu$ is the dipole moment. Symmetric modes of centrosymmetric molecules (e.g. CO₂ symmetric stretch) are IR inactive — use Raman spectroscopy to observe them.

Vibrational Energy Levels

In the harmonic oscillator approximation, vibrational energy levels are equally spaced:

$$E_v = h\tilde{\nu}\left(v + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad v = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

The fundamental IR absorption corresponds to $v = 0 \to 1$, observed at wavenumber $\tilde{\nu}$. Anharmonicity causes overtones ($v = 0 \to 2$) to appear at slightly less than $2\tilde{\nu}$.

Number of Normal Modes

For a molecule with $N$ atoms:

$$\text{Linear: } 3N - 5 \quad\quad \text{Nonlinear: } 3N - 6$$

e.g. CO₂ (linear, N=3): $3(3)-5 = 4$ modes (ν₂ doubly degenerate counts as 2). H₂O (nonlinear, N=3): $3(3)-6 = 3$ modes.
